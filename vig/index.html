The feel of a historic window

What People Respond to in Historic Windows (Even If They Can't Explain It)

Most people don't look at a historic window and think about offsets, profiles, or shadow lines.

They just know it feels right.

There's a depth to it. A weight. A sense that the window is part of the wall — not just applied to it.

Light doesn't just pass through it — it interacts with it. Edges catch shadow. Profiles create contrast. The glass sits back in a way that gives the entire opening dimension.

Modern windows tend to lose this. They become flat. Uniform. The proportions may be close — but visually, something is missing.

That "something" isn't one detail — it's the accumulation of many small, precise relationships:

  • How far the glass sits from the interior surface
  • How the exterior edge meets the light
  • The thickness and shape of the profiles
  • The way shadow defines the opening throughout the day

Most people can't name these things — but they recognize immediately when they're gone.

And once they're gone, they're very difficult to get back.

The compromise spectrum

The Problem: Performance Upgrades Change Those Relationships

Every modern "improvement" to a window changes the geometry that creates that look.

Every option on the market fits somewhere on that compromise spectrum:

Original glass

Storm windows

In practical terms:

VIG allows a 150-year-old window design to perform like a modern high-efficiency unit — without looking like one.

The technology

What is Vacuum Insulated Glass (VIG)?

At its simplest, VIG is:

1

Two Thin Lites

Typically 3–6mm

2

Vacuum Gap

A microscopic ~0.2mm gap

3

Micro-Spacers

Supported by micro-spacers (pillars)

4

Hermetic Seal

Hermetically sealed around the perimeter

The key difference is the vacuum.

There is nothing between the lites — no air, no gas — so there is virtually no medium to transfer heat.

This is what allows VIG to dramatically outperform traditional insulated glass while remaining extremely thin.

Why IG doesn't fit

Why Insulated Glass (IG) Doesn't Work in Historic Windows

Insulated glass improves thermal performance — but it does so by changing the very geometry that gives historic windows their character.

To perform well, IG requires:

What IG requires
¾"–1"
  • Large air gaps (approximately ½" to ¾")
  • Thick overall units (often ¾" to 1")
Historic windows rely on very specific relationships
⅞" / ⅜"
  • Approximately ⅞" interior glass offset
  • Approximately ⅜" exterior offset
  • Thin glass (approximately ⅛")

That thickness makes compromise unavoidable.

The incompatibility

When IG is introduced:

  • Those offsets must be reduced or redistributed
  • Profiles flatten
  • Shadow lines disappear

These sound like small changes — but they fundamentally alter how the window reads, especially from the interior.

What you'll notice

Comfort Near the Window (Why This Matters)

With traditional glass — and even many insulated glass units — the interior surface of the window gets cold in winter.

That cold surface chills the air next to it. The air drops, rolls across the floor, and creates what feels like a draft.

Most people assume the window is leaking.

It's not.

It's the glass.

Because VIG keeps the interior glass surface much closer to room temperature:

  • The "cold fall" effect is dramatically reduced
  • Air movement near the window is minimized
  • The space near the window feels stable — not drafty

In practical terms, this is the difference between avoiding the window in winter… and being able to sit right next to it comfortably.

Vent vs No Vent (Getter Systems)

There are two types of VIG:

Vent port

1. Vented (after-seal vacuum)

  • Small port near the corner (about the size of a dime)
  • Can be positioned out of normal sightlines
Internal getter strip

2. Non-vented (sealed in a vacuum chamber)

  • No visible port
  • Includes a "getter" (small internal strip or needle near the edge)

In practice:

  • Both are minimally visible
  • Both become unnoticeable once installed

Many historic homes include arched, round, or otherwise custom-shaped windows.

These openings are often defining architectural features — and they cannot be simplified without changing the character of the building.

Not all VIG can be produced in curved or custom shapes.

In some cases, this becomes the limiting factor in whether VIG can be used at all.

Some VIG products are simply not available in these geometries.

This is not a performance issue — it is a capability limitation.

Which means the decision becomes:

Not "Which performs best?"
But "Which can actually be made to fit the design?"

For projects involving arches, rounds, or custom geometries, this becomes a hard constraint.

We account for this early — selecting glass systems that can meet both the performance requirements and the geometry of the original design.

Divided lites

Simulated Divided Lite (SDL) vs True Divided Lite (TDL)

Traditional windows used true divided lite construction, with individual panes separated by muntins.

Most modern windows use simulated divided lite (SDL).

With insulated glass, SDL often falls short:

  • Spacer bars reveal the separation
  • Reflections give it away
  • Muntin proportions are altered

With VIG, those limitations largely disappear.

Because the gap between lites is microscopic:

  • The glass reads as a single pane
  • There is no visible spacer bar between muntins
  • Reflections and sightlines remain consistent

Combined with our muntin construction methods, this allows SDL that is visually indistinguishable from true divided lite.

In real-world evaluation:

At a Historic Preservation Commission trade event, architects reviewing our windows were unable to distinguish SDL from true divided lite.

In another project, an experienced historic contractor told the homeowner they believed they had purchased true divided lite windows — when in fact they were our SDL.

These are not showroom comparisons. They are field observations from professionals who work with historic windows regularly.

Sourcing

VIG Manufacturers: What Actually Matters

Not all VIG is the same.

Key differences include:

  • Thermal performance
  • Edge seal durability
  • Spacer visibility
  • Optical clarity and coatings
  • Reliability of supply

Lead times vary significantly:

  • Some imported products require 12–16+ weeks
  • Domestic production is typically 2–4 weeks

This becomes critical in real projects. We evaluate suppliers based on:

  • Real-world durability
  • Visual performance once installed
  • Compatibility with traditional construction
  • Confidence in delivery

Almost none are designed with historic appearance as a priority. That distinction matters.
